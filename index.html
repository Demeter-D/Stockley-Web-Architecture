The hybrid channel-manager stack

A buildable architecture for the cabin & food site: Next.js and Payload for the site and content, a certified channel manager for the calendar and the Airbnb / Booking.com sync nobody can build alone, Stripe for direct payment.

Overview

Airbnb and Booking.com don't hand out self-serve APIs — that access is reserved for a small set of certified Channel Manager / PMS partners. So the calendar and OTA sync aren't something you build; they're something you buy. Everything else — the brand, the content, the checkout — you own outright.

The split: Next.js + Payload CMS is your site, under your control, editable by staff without a developer. Lodgify (or a peer) is the piece that actually talks to Airbnb and Booking.com, holds the live calendar, and prevents double-booking across channels. Stripe takes the payment. The three meet at one seam: your site calls the channel manager for a quote, takes payment, then tells the channel manager to confirm the reservation.

Booking flow

The channel manager's quote comes with a short hold — treat that hold, not your own logic, as what actually prevents a double-booking. Your app's job is to get from "guest wants these dates" to "reservation confirmed" before that hold expires.

Fig. 02 — Direct booking, cabin checkout

sequenceDiagram
  participant G as Guest
  participant W as Next.js
  participant C as Channel manager
  participant S as Stripe
  participant A as App server

  G->>W: Select cabin + dates
  W->>C: Get availability & rate
  C-->>W: Quote + hold (~15 min)
  G->>W: Confirm details
  W->>S: Create Checkout Session
  S-->>G: Hosted Checkout
  G->>S: Pay
  S-->>A: webhook: checkout.session.completed
  A->>C: Create reservation
  C-->>A: Reservation confirmed
  C--)Airbnb: Push calendar block
  C--)BookingCom: Push calendar block
  A->>A: Write local booking record
  A-->>G: Confirmation email

Dining events skip the channel manager entirely — a Payload Events entry with a Stripe price behind it is enough; there's no OTA calendar to keep in sync for a table reservation.

Vendor choice

This is the decision with the most gravity in the whole stack — moving calendars between channel managers later means re-listing on every OTA. Worth a short bake-off before committing.

Risks & tradeoffs

  • The hold window is borrowed, not owned. The ~15-minute quote hold is enforced by the channel manager, not your code. Confirm its exact TTL before launch, and treat the channel manager's reservation webhook — not the Stripe webhook — as what actually means "booked."
  • Webhooks get dropped. Verify Stripe signatures, make the reservation-creation handler idempotent since retries happen, and reconcile nightly against the channel manager's API in case an event never arrives.
  • The calendar is the hard-to-reverse piece. Switching channel managers later means re-listing on every OTA from scratch. Everything else in this stack — the CMS, the frontend — can be replaced without anyone outside your team noticing.
  • Three sources of truth, one boundary. Channel manager owns calendar and rates. Payload owns content. Postgres owns booking history. Keep it that way — the moment someone edits a rate inside the CMS instead of the channel manager, the two go out of sync.
